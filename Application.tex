\section*{Application to Strong Heart Study}

\subsection*{Study population and exposures}

We applied g-BKMR to data from the Strong Heart Study \citep[SHS;][]{shs_lee_1990}, a prospective cohort of American Indian adults with low to moderate exposure to metals and a high burden of cardiometabolic disease. The analytic sample consisted of 679 participants with complete urinary metal measurements at three study visits (Visits 1--3) and fasting blood glucose measured at Visit 3. Six urinary metals were considered: selenium (Se), cadmium (Cd), molybdenum (Mo), tungsten (W), arsenic (As), and zinc (Zn), all standardised to urinary creatinine and log-transformed prior to analysis. The mixture comprised 18 exposure variables (six metals $\times$ three visits).

The outcome was log-transformed fasting blood glucose at Visit~3 (motivated by right-skewed distributions; skewness $1.8$--$2.1$; see Supplementary Figure~1). Time-varying confounders included waist circumference and fasting glucose measured at Visits 1 and 2, modeled as time-dependent covariates affected by prior exposure. Baseline covariates included age, sex, body mass index, study centre, smoking status (current/former/never), and baseline kidney function (CKD-EPI eGFR). Missing values were handled by multiple imputation ($m = 5$, predictive mean matching). The directed acyclic graph encoding the assumed causal structure is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:dag}; the cross-timepoint correlation matrix is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:corr}.

\subsection*{Model specification and estimation}

We denote the six-metal exposure vector at visit $t$ by $A_t = (A_{t,1}, \ldots, A_{t,6})$ for $t = 0, 1, 2$ (corresponding to Visits 1--3), the time-varying confounders at visit $t$ by $L_t = (\text{waist}_t, \text{glucose}_t)$ for $t = 1, 2$, the baseline covariates by $C_0$, and the outcome by $Y$ (log fasting glucose at Visit~3). Figure~\ref{fig:dag} shows the assumed causal structure: prior exposures $A_{<t}$ affect both current confounders $L_t$ and future exposures $A_t$, and all paths converge on $Y$.

The g-BKMR framework fits a sequence of BKMR models to implement the g-formula. Specifically, five separate BKMR models were fit:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Mediator models} ($T - 1 = 2$ time points $\times$ 2 confounders $= 4$ models): at each visit $t \in \{1, 2\}$, waist circumference and fasting glucose were each regressed on all prior exposures $(A_0, \ldots, A_{t-1})$ and all prior mediator values $(L_1, \ldots, L_{t-1})$ through a Gaussian kernel, with baseline covariates $C_0$ entering the model parametrically. For example, the mediator model for $L_1 = (\text{waist}_1, \text{glucose}_1)$ uses $Z = A_0$ (6 exposure columns), while the model for $L_2$ uses $Z = (A_0, A_1, L_1)$ (6 + 6 + 2 = 14 columns).
    \item \textbf{Outcome model} (1 model): log glucose at Visit~3 was regressed on the full exposure history $(A_0, A_1, A_2)$ and all mediator values $(L_1, L_2)$ through a Gaussian kernel (18 + 4 = 22 columns in $Z$), with baseline covariates entering parametrically.
\end{enumerate}
All exposure and mediator variables were standardised to zero mean and unit variance before entering the kernel. Both the outcome $Y$ and all exposure components $Z$ were also standardised. All models used component-wise variable selection \citep{bobb_bkmr_2015}, which assigns an independent posterior inclusion probability (PIP) to each column of $Z$, thereby identifying the most relevant metal-timepoint combinations. Each model was fit with $24{,}000$ MCMC iterations using a Gaussian kernel with 50 knots (placed via a space-filling design on the scaled exposure matrix). The baseline covariates $C_0$ comprised age, sex, BMI, study centre (dummy-coded: AZ, OK, SD), smoking status (current/former/never, dummy-coded), and CKD-EPI eGFR. Constant and collinear columns were removed via QR decomposition before fitting, yielding $d$ baseline covariate columns that entered each model parametrically alongside the kernel term. The Visit~3 values of waist and glucose used in the outcome model's $Z$ matrix were imputed via last-observation-carried-forward from Visit~2 (or Visit~1 if Visit~2 was missing), since Visit~3 glucose is the outcome itself.

Causal effects were estimated via Monte Carlo g-computation. For each of 40 retained posterior draws (iterations $22{,}000$ to $24{,}000$, every 50th), we simulated $K = 1{,}000$ counterfactual trajectories under two static exposure regimes:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\mathbf{a}$: all 18 metal-timepoint exposures set to their 25th sample percentile (reference);
    \item $\mathbf{a}^*$: all 18 exposures set to a target percentile $q$.
\end{itemize}
For each posterior draw $j$ and each Monte Carlo replicate $k$, counterfactual mediators were sampled sequentially forward in time: first $L_1^{(a)}$ was predicted from \texttt{bkmr::SamplePred} given $A_0 = a_0$ and baseline covariates set to their sample mean; then $L_2^{(a)}$ was predicted given $(A_0, A_1, L_1^{(a)})$. The same procedure was applied under regime $\mathbf{a}^*$. Finally, the outcome $Y^{(a)}$ was predicted given the full counterfactual history $(A_0, A_1, A_2, L_1^{(a)}, L_2^{(a)})$, and similarly for $Y^{(a^*)}$. The average causal effect was computed as
\[
    \widehat{\text{ACE}}(q) = \frac{1}{|\text{sel}|} \sum_j \frac{1}{K} \sum_k \bigl[ Y_j^{(a^*), k} - Y_j^{(a), k} \bigr],
\]
with 95\% credible intervals obtained from the 2.5th and 97.5th percentiles of the pooled posterior draws across all five multiply imputed datasets.

We targeted two estimands: (i) the \textit{joint mixture causal effect}, defined as $\widehat{\text{ACE}}(q)$ for $q \in \{0.30, 0.35, \ldots, 0.90\}$ (yielding a causal dose-response curve), and (ii) \textit{single-variable causal effects}, defined as the effect of shifting one metal at one visit from its 25th to 75th percentile while holding all other exposures at their 50th percentile.

\subsection*{Results}

The joint mixture causal effect on log fasting glucose followed a positive, approximately monotonic dose-response (Figure~\ref{fig:dr-joint}). The 95\% credible interval excluded zero above approximately the 45th percentile, with the magnitude of the effect increasing through the upper end of the exposure distribution. At the 75th vs.\ 25th percentile contrast, the average causal effect was $1.01$ standardised units of log glucose (95\% CrI: $0.72$, $1.33$). Examining each metal-timepoint individually (Figure~\ref{fig:forest}), zinc was the dominant contributor to the joint mixture signal: the IQR causal effect of Zn was $0.51$ SD at Visit~1 (95\% CrI: $0.28$, $0.75$), $0.26$ SD at Visit~2 ($0.03$, $0.51$), and $0.36$ SD at Visit~3 ($0.20$, $0.55$). All three Zn-timepoint effects were credibly distinct from zero. Selenium at Visit~1 also showed a positive association ($0.19$ SD; 95\% CrI: $-0.01$, $0.36$; posterior probability of a positive effect: $0.97$). Cadmium at Visit~1 showed an inverse association ($-0.21$ SD; 95\% CrI: $-0.37$, $-0.05$). The remaining metal-timepoints (Mo, W, As, and the later visits of Se and Cd) had effect estimates with credible intervals overlapping zero. Per-metal dose-response curves are shown in Supplementary Figure~2.

\subsection*{Sensitivity analyses}

We conducted two sensitivity analyses to assess the robustness of the main findings. First, to evaluate whether the metal--glucose associations were driven by participants with established diabetes, we excluded the 265 participants with baseline diabetes (WHO clinical definition; analytic sample $n = 414$). In this subsample, the joint mixture dose-response was substantially attenuated, with the 95\% credible interval covering zero across most quantile contrasts (Figure~\ref{fig:dr-compare}). Single-variable effects were also markedly reduced (Supplementary Figure~3): Zn(V1) decreased from $0.51$ to $0.01$ SD, Cd(V1) reversed sign and became non-significant, and most other estimates moved toward the null. Two findings remained robust: Se(V1) remained positive and credibly above zero ($0.07$ SD; 95\% CrI: $0.004$, $0.15$), and Zn(V3) retained a borderline positive signal ($0.14$ SD; 95\% CrI: $0.00$, $0.35$).

Second, to address potential residual confounding by smoking intensity---particularly relevant to the inverse Cd(V1) finding---we additionally adjusted for baseline pack-years alongside the categorical smoking-status variable. For never-smokers, pack-years was set to zero prior to multiple imputation. All findings were robust to this adjustment (Supplementary Figures~5--7): the joint dose-response at the 75th vs.\ 25th percentile was $1.06$ SD (95\% CrI: $0.80$, $1.39$), compared with $1.01$ ($0.72$, $1.33$) in the main analysis. Notably, the inverse Cd(V1) effect was essentially unchanged ($-0.22$ SD; 95\% CrI: $-0.35$, $-0.10$), indicating that this association is not explained by smoking intensity alone.
